## Enhanced Knowledge: Market Sizing Analysis [startup-business-analyst/market-sizing-analysis]
- Three-tier framework: TAM (total revenue at 100% share), SAM (targetable with current product, filtered by geography/segment/capability), SOM (realistic 3-5 year capture, typically 2-5% for new entrants)
- Top-down: TAM from industry reports -> apply geographic % -> segment % -> SAM; data from Gartner, Forrester, IDC, Statista, public filings
- Bottom-up (most credible for investors): define segments, count potential customers, multiply by annual revenue per customer; requires customer research
- Value theory (for new categories): quantify problem cost, calculate value of solution, estimate willingness to pay (10-30% of value), multiply by customer base
- SAM filters: geographic constraints, product limitations, customer requirements (size/industry/use case), distribution access, regulatory restrictions
- SOM calculation: conservative Year 3 = SAM x 2%, Year 5 = SAM x 5%; account for competition, resources, GTM effectiveness
- Validation: triangulate top-down vs. bottom-up (within 30%), check against public company revenues, sense-check pricing, review with experts
- Red flags: TAM too small (<$1B for VC), TAM too large (unsupported), SOM too aggressive (>10% in 5 years), methodology inconsistency (>50% gap)
- Industry-specific: SaaS (companies x ACV x expansion), Marketplace (GMV x take rate), Consumer (users x ARPU x frequency), B2B (companies x deal size x deals/year)

## Enhanced Knowledge: Competitive Landscape [startup-business-analyst/competitive-landscape]
- Porter's Five Forces: New Entrants (barriers: capital, scale, switching costs, network effects), Supplier Power (concentration, substitutes), Buyer Power (concentration, differentiation, price sensitivity), Substitutes (alternatives, switching cost), Rivalry (competitor count, growth rate, differentiation)
- Blue Ocean Strategy: Four Actions Framework -- Eliminate (taken-for-granted factors), Reduce (below standard), Raise (above standard), Create (never offered); strategy canvas maps offerings on key factors
- Positioning map: plot competitors on 2 key dimensions (e.g., Price vs Features, Complexity vs Ease), identify white space gaps, validate gap = real customer need
- Differentiation: product (features, performance, UX), service (support, onboarding), brand (trust, community), price (premium, value, transparent)
- Positioning statement: For [target] who [need], our product is [category] that [benefit], unlike [competitor], our product [differentiation]
- Competitive intelligence sources: websites, press releases, job postings (strategy hints), G2/Capterra reviews, Glassdoor, SEC filings, patents, customer interviews, win/loss analysis
- Sustainable advantages: network effects (value increases with users), switching costs (high change cost), economies of scale, brand, proprietary tech, regulatory barriers
- Monitoring cadence: weekly (product releases, news), monthly (win/loss, positioning), quarterly (deep review, strategy adjustment), annually (major reassessment)

## Enhanced Knowledge: Startup Financial Modeling [startup-business-analyst/startup-financial-modeling]
- Revenue model: cohort-based -- MRR = sum(Cohort Size x Retention Rate x ARPU); track New MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churned = Net New MRR
- Cost structure: COGS (hosting, payment processing, support), S&M (CAC, advertising, sales comp), R&D (engineering, product, design), G&A (exec, finance, legal, office)
- Cash flow: beginning cash + inflows (revenue, funding) - outflows (opex, capex) = ending cash; Runway = Cash / Monthly Burn
- Headcount: fully-loaded = salary x 1.35; typical early SaaS split: Engineering 40-50%, S&M 25-35%, G&A 10-15%, CS 5-10%
- Three scenarios: Conservative P10 (slower acquisition, higher churn), Base P50 (realistic, primary planning), Optimistic P90 (faster growth, better unit economics)
- Time horizon: monthly detail Years 1-2, quarterly Year 3, annual Years 4-5
- SaaS benchmarks: gross margin 75-85%, S&M 40-60% of revenue early stage, CAC payback <12 months, net retention 100-120%
- Fundraising: Pre-Money + Investment = Post-Money; Dilution = Investment / Post-Money; use of funds breakdown (product, S&M, G&A, working capital); ensure runway to next milestone + 6 months buffer
- Sanity checks: 3x growth Year 2 / 2x Year 3, LTV/CAC >3, burn multiple <2.0, revenue per employee growing, gross margin appropriate for model
- Common pitfalls: overly optimistic revenue, underestimating costs (add 20% buffer), ignoring cash flow timing (revenue != cash), static headcount (hiring takes 3-6 months, ramp 3-6 months)

## Enhanced Knowledge: Startup Metrics Framework [startup-business-analyst/startup-metrics-framework]
- Universal metrics: MRR/ARR, growth rate (seed: 15-20% MoM, Series A: 3-5x YoY), CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC (>3.0 healthy), CAC payback (<12mo excellent, <18mo good), burn rate, runway (maintain 12-18 months)
- Burn multiple = Net Burn / Net New ARR; <1.0 exceptional, 1.0-1.5 good, 1.5-2.0 acceptable, >2.0 inefficient
- SaaS-specific: Net Dollar Retention (>120% best-in-class, >100% good), Gross Retention (>90% excellent), Magic Number = Net New ARR / prior quarter S&M (>0.75 efficient), Rule of 40 = Growth% + Margin% (>40% excellent), Quick Ratio = (New+Expansion) / (Churn+Contraction) (>4.0 healthy)
- Marketplace: GMV, take rate (10-30% typical), fill rate (>80% strong liquidity), repeat rate (>60% strong retention), supply/demand balance
- Consumer/mobile: DAU/MAU ratio (>50% exceptional), Day 1/7/30 retention, K-factor = invites x conversion (>1.0 viral)
- B2B: win rate (20-40%), sales cycle length (SMB 30-60d, mid 60-120d, enterprise 120-270d), pipeline coverage (3-5x quota), ACV
- Stage focus: pre-seed (retention, engagement, qualitative), seed (MRR growth, baseline CAC/LTV, gross retention), Series A (ARR growth, unit economics, NDR, burn multiple, magic number)
- Investor presentation: current value + growth rate/trend + context (target, benchmark); avoid vanity metrics (total users without retention)
- Common mistakes: vanity metrics, too many metrics (track 5-7 intensely), ignoring unit economics, not segmenting, gaming metrics

## Enhanced Knowledge: KPI Dashboard Design [business-analytics/kpi-dashboard-design]
- KPI framework levels: Strategic (long-term, monthly/quarterly, executives), Tactical (department, weekly/monthly, managers), Operational (day-to-day, real-time/daily, teams)
- SMART KPIs: Specific definition, Measurable quantification, Achievable targets, Relevant to goals, Time-bound period
- Dashboard hierarchy: Executive Summary (4-6 headline KPIs, trends, alerts) -> Department Views (Sales, Marketing, Product, Finance) -> Detailed Drilldowns (individual metrics, root cause)
- Common KPIs: Sales (MRR, ARR, pipeline value, win rate, deal size), Marketing (CPA, CAC, MQLs, conversion rate), Product (DAU/MAU, NPS, feature adoption, churn), Finance (gross margin, EBITDA, cash flow, revenue per employee)
- Layout patterns: Executive Summary (4 metric cards + trend chart + breakdown chart + alerts), SaaS Metrics (MRR/ARR cards + growth line + unit economics + cohort heatmap + churn breakdown), Real-time Operations (system health gauges + service status + throughput sparkline + error rate + alerts feed)
- SQL patterns: MRR calculation with subscription interval normalization, cohort retention with date_trunc and age extraction, CAC with marketing spend / new customers
- Visualization: Streamlit/Plotly for interactive dashboards; metric_card with value + delta arrow + percentage change; cohort heatmap with color scale; pie chart with hole for product breakdown
- Best practices: limit to 5-7 KPIs, show context (comparisons, trends, targets), consistent colors (red=bad, green=good), enable drilldown, match update frequency to metric type
- Don'ts: vanity metrics, overcrowded layouts (use white space), 3D charts (distort perception), hidden methodology, ignore mobile responsiveness
